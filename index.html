Exercise Programming & Training Q&A

What are the three unbreakable rules of a good program? â–¶

1. Progressive Overload

  • You must get 1% better regularly (doesn't have to be every session)
  • Can be: more weight, more reps, more sets, better technique, longer time under tension
  • Without progression, your body won't change

2. Flexible Periodization

  • Your program should adapt to your daily needs
  • Life stress, sleep quality, recovery state matter
  • You don't have to do exactly what's written if you feel terrible
  • Adjusting intensity/volume on the fly keeps you consistent long-term

3. Consistency

  • More important than program design, rep ranges, or exercises
  • Pick something YOU will actually do
  • Momentum from doing the program matters more than perfect programming

How do I increase intensity without adding weight? â–¶

Intensity Techniques (Progressive Overload Without New Weight):

  1. Add Reps: If you did 8 reps last week, do 9 this week with same weight
  2. Add Sets: Start with 3 sets, progress to 4 sets over weeks
  3. Decrease Rest Time: Reduce 90 seconds to 75 seconds between sets
  4. Improve Technique: Better form = more muscle engagement
  5. Time Under Tension: Slow 3-second lower, 1-second pause, 1-second press = more difficulty
  6. Range of Motion: Go deeper/fuller range = harder stimulus

Smart Progression: Mix these tactics. Don't try to use all 6 at onceâ€”pick 1â€“2 per week and rotate.

Does it matter what order I do exercises in? â–¶

YESâ€”Exercise Order Affects Performance.

Best Practice: Compound Lifts First

  • When you're fresh, your CNS is primed for heavy, complex movements
  • Doing isolation first leaves you fatigued for compounds (wastes potential strength gains)
  • Example Workout Order:

  • 2) Incline Dumbbell Press (secondary compound)
  • 3) Cable Flyes (isolation)
  • 4) Dips (accessory)

Should I train cardio and strength on the same day? â–¶

Short Answer: Yes, but with strategy.

If Your Goal is Muscle Gain:

  • Do strength first (when CNS is fresh), cardio second
  • Keep cardio moderate: 20â€“30 min at Zone 2 intensity
  • Avoid excessive cardio (>5 hours/week) as it can interfere with recovery

If Your Goal is Fat Loss:

  • Still prioritize strength first
  • Moderate cardio is fine and helps with caloric deficit
  • Example: 4 strength days + 2-3 cardio sessions per week

If Your Goal is Cardiovascular Fitness:

  • Do cardio first (when energy is highest) or on separate days
  • Strength training should be maintenance-level (2x/week)

Practical Option: Separate them if possible. Strength one day, cardio another. Your body will thank you with better recovery.

Source: Brad Schoenfeld, Eric Helms

Why do my calorie needs vary? â–¶

Multiple Factors Affect Daily Burn:

  • Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR): Calories you burn at rest (varies by age, gender, muscle mass)

  • Adaptive Thermogenesis: Your body adapts to prolonged deficitsâ€”metabolism slows slightly
  • Hormones & Stress: Cortisol, sleep, menstrual cycle all affect calorie expenditure

Why This Matters: The calculator gives you a starting point. Track your weight for 2-3 weeks. If it's not changing as expected, adjust by Â±100-200 calories.

Should I eat the same macros every single day? â–¶

Not Required, But Helpful for Consistency.

Why Some Variation is Fine:

  • Your body averages intake over weeks, not days
  • Some days you might want higher carbs (before hard workouts)
  • Some days higher fat (on rest days, lower activity)
  • Total weekly intake matters more than daily perfection

Practical Approach:

  • Training Days: Higher carbs (around workouts), moderate fat
  • Rest Days: Slightly lower carbs, slightly higher fat (still hitting protein)
  • Total Weekly Calories & Protein: Should be consistent

What if I miscounted calories and went over? â–¶

One High-Calorie Day Won't Ruin Your Progress.

The Math: To gain 1 pound of fat, you need a 3,500 calorie surplus. One meal at +500 calories won't do it.

What Actually Happens:

  • Extra calories = extra water retention and food in your stomach (temporary weight)
  • This reverses within 3-5 days if you return to normal intake
  • Your weight scale can fluctuate 2-5 pounds daily from sodium, glycogen, and hydration

Smart Response:

  • Don't "compensate" by eating less the next day (creates inconsistency)
  • Just return to normal tomorrow
  • One meal doesn't define your week or month
  • Focus on consistency over perfection

Long-Term Perspective: People who obsess over single days tend to binge-restrict cycles. People who are flexible with Â±100 calories typically see better results.

Source: Alan Aragon, Layne Norton
